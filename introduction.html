Introduction

The original goal of artificial intelligence (AI) was to create human-like intelligence in silico. Modern AI, while useful and instances of (real) intelligence, are (for the most part) nothing like human intelligence.

Despite this broadening of the definition of AI, there are still some people who have tried to reserve the name AI for artificial forms of human-like intelligence. But I think it is too late for that. Today the name AI is used as a name for a lot of non-human-like artificial intelligences.

Examples of non-human-like artificial intelligences that get called AI include: support vector machines (SVMs), k-mediods, decision trees, cascade-correlation, echo state networks, and many many others.

In contrast, the type of AI you tend to see and read in science-fiction movies and literature is often human-like. For example, the fictional character Data from the TV series Star Trek, the fictional character Sonny from the movie I, Robot, the fictional characters Agent Smith, Agent Brown & Agent Jones from the movie The Matrix were all fictional examples of human-like AI.

Non-human-like AIs have daily use in industry. The type of AI, in online advertising technology, which decides what is the "best" ad to show someone, is not human-like. The type of AI that you find in video games is not human-like. The type of AI that powers SPAM filters is not human-like.

And if you study AI in school, that type of AI will (almost certainly) not be human-like.

The interest of this book is artificial human-like intelligence. Some call this strong AI, others call this artificial general intelligence (AGI), while others call it cognitive AI.

The title of this book "Stronger AI" is a play on one of those names: strong AI.
